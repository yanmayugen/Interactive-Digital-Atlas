üìöProject Overview

This digital humanities project maps and analyzes 247 Han-language Islamic texts (Êº¢Ë™û„Ç§„Çπ„É©„Éº„É†ÊñáÁåÆ) that researchers have documented in Japanese and other collections. These books were published in China during the late 19th and early 20th centuries. The visualizations map the spatial distribution, temporal patterns, and circulation of these documented books across 12 Chinese publishing cities, with particular attention to their preservation in Japanese institutional repositories. This represents an ongoing initiative to locate, catalog, and make accessible more of these rare sources that have been dispersed globally.

This project is part of a broader research initiative studying the ethnic and religious identity formation of Chinese Muslims (Hui people, historically called Huimin ÂõûÊ∞ë) and their networks during the transition from imperial to Republican China. The Han-language Islamic texts‚Äîwritten by Hui intellectuals who translated and explained Islamic teachings using Chinese language and concepts‚Äîare key sources for understanding how this community maintained religious identity while living among the Han Chinese majority. Many of these texts were collected by Japanese military, diplomatic, and religious personnel active in China during the early 20th century and are now preserved in Japanese research institutions.

The project team collected bibliographic data about these books from Japanese institutional and private collections (including research visits to France). The dataset integrates bibliographic records with expert-curated metadata (58 fields) and scholarly annotations, providing insights into Hui Muslim intellectual networks, publishing patterns, and the transnational movement of these texts from China to Japan.

üîçUnderstanding the Documented Books

These visualizations reveal patterns in the production, circulation, and preservation of the Chinese Islamic texts that members of this project have documented so far. Understanding these patterns helps scholars locate these sources and recognize their availability for research. This project continues to seek and document additional Han-language Islamic texts held in collections worldwide.

üìç Publishing Concentration in Beijing and Urban Centers

The maps show Beijing's position as a major center of Hui Muslim publishing, accounting for 65% of documented books (120 out of 185 geocoded titles). Starting in the late 19th century, Hui Muslim intellectuals in Beijing, Tianjin, Shanghai, Shenyang, and Chengdu began publishing accessible texts for general readers, rather than the complex philosophical works that earlier generations had produced for scholars. This shift in publishing activity created networks through which books circulated beyond their places of publication, reaching Hui Muslim communities throughout China. The maps help scholars identify major publishing centers and understand the geographic distribution of these texts.

üåè Collection and Preservation by Japanese Institutions

95% of the books with known current locations are now preserved in Japanese institutions, with Tenri University holding 195 books (the largest collection). These texts were collected during the early 20th century when Japanese military personnel, diplomats, religious figures, and nationalists were actively gathering information about Hui Muslims as part of operations in Beijing, Tianjin, and Inner Mongolia. The network map traces these collection patterns, showing how texts produced across China came to be concentrated in Japanese repositories. For researchers, especially those based in China where religious censorship has made accessing these materials difficult, the Japanese collections represent crucial research resources. The visualizations serve as a guide to locating these sources.

üé® Geographic Patterns in Text Production

The thematic maps show distinct patterns of regional publishing. Beijing and other urban centers saw the development of modern print enterprises producing accessible texts for general readership. Yunnan and Sichuan provinces, regions with established Hui Muslim populations, continued producing traditional Islamic learning materials. The Hui people (descendants of Muslim immigrants from West, Central, and Southeast Asia who arrived in China between the 7th and 14th centuries) developed the tradition of Han-language Islamic texts as a way to maintain religious identity while living surrounded by Han Chinese. These texts translated Arabic and Persian religious works using Confucian, Buddhist, and Taoist terminology. The geographic differentiation visible in the maps reflects how Islamic text production adapted to different local contexts and audiences across China's diverse regions.

‚è∞ Historical Context and Publishing Timeline

The 80-year span (1863-1943) of publishing activity captured in the dataset encompasses transformations in Chinese history: late Qing reforms, the 1911 revolution, the warlord period, and eventual Japanese invasion. Han-language Islamic texts from the 17th-19th centuries were complex philosophical works that general believers found difficult to read. Starting in the late 19th century, a new type of accessible enlightenment text appeared, including practical guides, primers, and educational materials. These circulated through Hui Muslim religious, cultural, and political networks beyond their places of publication, contributing to heightened ethnic and religious consciousness among the Hui community. The end date of 1943 marks the disruption caused by wartime conditions. Understanding this timeline helps contextualize individual works within broader historical changes in Hui Muslim communities.

üñ®Ô∏è Production Methods and Physical Characteristics

The books show various production methods from this period, including moveable type Chinese and Arabic text (sometimes reproduced by offset printing), hand-written calligraphy, and offset printing of pages composed of both type and calligraphy. Some books feature Arabic text with Chinese transliteration and translation. The physical descriptions in the metadata include information about editors, Arabic calligraphers, translators, and publication locations, providing insights into the networks of individuals involved in producing these works.

üó∫Ô∏è An Ongoing Initiative to Locate and Make Rare Sources Accessible

A primary goal of these visualizations is to make rare Han-language Islamic texts known and accessible to the scholarly community, and to support an ongoing initiative to locate additional examples of these books. Since the late 2010s, the Chinese government has intensified censorship of Islamic-related texts, making research in China difficult and sometimes dangerous. The Japanese collections therefore represent crucial research resources, but many of these texts are not widely cataloged in Western library systems. By mapping the locations, content types, publication patterns, and preservation institutions of documented books, these visualizations serve as both a discovery tool and a foundation for future collecting efforts. Researchers studying Hui Muslim history, Republican-era ethnic and religious policy, Sino-Japanese relations, or minority cultural preservation can use these maps to identify sources for their work. The project aims to continue locating additional texts, create digital catalogs and databases, and further facilitate access to these materials. This ongoing work contributes to revitalizing Chinese Islamic studies and providing historical perspectives on contemporary issues of ethnic and religious minorities in China.

üî¨ Digital Mapping Methods

These visualizations show how GIS and network analysis tools can be applied to book history and collection mapping. By spatializing bibliographic data, temporal patterns and institutional connections become visible in ways that differ from traditional catalog listings. The multi-layered approach‚Äîcombining spatial distribution, temporal spans, thematic categorization, publisher networks, and migration flows‚Äîallows scholars to examine how these books were produced and preserved. This approach can be applied to other collections, opening research directions in Asian book history and transnational book circulation.

üåç Research Questions and Future Directions

These visualizations open research questions for scholars. How did the publication and circulation of accessible Han-language Islamic texts in the late 19th and early 20th centuries affect Hui Muslim ethnic and religious identity? What networks‚Äîreligious, cultural, and political‚Äîenabled these texts to circulate beyond their places of publication? How did Japanese collection activities relate to their military and diplomatic operations in China, and what responses did these activities elicit from Hui Muslims? The visualizations also reveal gaps‚Äîcertain regions, time periods, or text types that may be underrepresented. Identifying these patterns and gaps can inform future research on Hui Muslim intellectual history, early Chinese Nationalist and Communist ethnic and religious policies, Sino-Japanese relations during this period, and broader questions about how ethnic and religious minority communities maintain identity and cultural practices. Making these sources visible supports scholarship that can contribute to understanding multicultural coexistence in both historical and contemporary contexts.
